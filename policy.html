Policy

As there are lots of fake news, misinformation, disinformation, it is important to understand how to deal with those message properly.

The fake information not only affecting the public, it also affecting the trust of government and the whole socity. As a result, the government should take action to control those fake message, such as...

Legislate

There are some examples of different countries' legislate:

  • Singapore launched 'Protection from Online Falsehoods and Manipulation Act (POFMA) in 2 October, 2019.

    POFMA stipulates that individuals shall not, either within or outside Singapore, disseminate statements that are prejudicial to the security of Singapore, or to public health, public safety, public tranquillity or public finances, or to the friendly relations between Singapore and other countries, or to the results of a presidential election, general election of members of Parliament, by-election of members of Parliament, or referendum, or that excite feelings of enmity, hatred or ill-will between individuals or races, or that diminish the public's confidence in the Government, organ of state and statutory boards in the performance of their duties or the exercise of their powers. (b) any statement that arouses feelings of enmity, hatred or ill-will between individuals or races, or reduces public confidence in the government, organ of state and statutory boards in the performance of their duties or the exercise of their powers.

    Protection from Online Falsehoods and Manipulation Act
    Situation Punishment
    Individuals who have violated this law and used fake accounts or software as a means of dissemination. The maximum penalty is a fine of SGD $100,000 (approximately HKD $580000) or 10 years' imprisonment.
    A software manufacturer or service provider who commits an offence Is liable to a fine of up to SGD 60,000 (approximately HKD $348000) or to imprisonment for up to six years.
    Provided that in no case shall the said penalty exceed a maximum penalty of SGD$1 million.

  • In Germany, The Social Network Compulsory Law was passed in 2017 to regulate speech on online social media platforms.

    The Social Network Compulsory Law
    Situation Punishment
    Upon receipt of a report, social media platforms are required to remove clearly illegal content, including hate speech, malicious defamation or incitement to violence, within 24 hours. Failure to do so may result in a fine of more than 5 million (approximately HK$46.58 million) or less than 50 million (approximately HK$440 million).

Supervision

There are some example of different countries' supervision:

  • In 2022, the police of Hong Kong introduced a "24-hour public opinion tracking mechanism" to monitor fake news online and offline through co-operation with technology companies and the use of big data.

  • Apart from legislation, the European Union ("EU") started to introduce the Code of Practice on Disinformation ("Code") in 2018 to encourage IT companies to voluntarily sign up and commit to self-regulation to tackle the problem of false information. Many large IT companies, such as Google and facebook, have signed the Code of Practice on Disinformation and committed to self-regulation to tackle the problem of false information on their platforms. A number of IT companies that have signed the code have also introduced new policies to improve their services as pledged, including co-operation with fact-checking organisations and promotion of users' media literacy.
